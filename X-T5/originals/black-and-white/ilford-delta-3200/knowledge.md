# Ilford Delta 3200 — Grade Analysis & Derivation

> Original (datasheet + scan validated). See [`recipe.md`](recipe.md), the [B&W README](../README.md), and the [Knowledge base](../../../../Knowledge/README.md).

## The film
Ilford Delta 3200 Professional, the brand's flagship high-speed push stock. The datasheet (archived: [`_reference-sources/datasheets/ilford/delta-3200.pdf`](../../../_reference-sources/datasheets/README.md)) reveals a fact most people don't know: its true ISO speed is 1000, not 3200, it's *designed* to be metered and shot at EI 3200 with extended development (usable range EI 400–25000, "particularly recommended" 1600–6400). This is a push-process film by design, not a fast film that happens to push well.

## Real-scan confirmation (1 image, analysis-only)
An overcast street scene (a man at a bus stop, UK high street): prominent, obvious grain visible even in the flat overcast sky, heavier and more textured than either Tri-X or HP5 in our other real scans; moderate contrast with the day's flat light; shadow detail well retained in the man's coat and the shop doorways despite the grain load. Consistent with a push-developed high-speed emulsion: grain-forward but not contrast-starved.

## The grade in one line
The grainiest B&W in the bank, a "shoot it in the dark" push-speed stock with a smoother, more organic pushed tonality than Kodak's equivalent.

## Delta 3200 vs Kodak T-Max P3200: a deliberate distinction
Both are true-ISO-~1000 stocks designed for EI 3200 push development, direct competitors. Community/reviewer consensus (well-documented) describes Delta 3200 as slightly smoother and more "creamy" in midtone rendering, where T-Max P3200 reads snappier and more clinical. This recipe reflects that: Highlight 0 / Shadow +2 / Sharpness 0 / Clarity +1 (Delta) vs Highlight +1 / Shadow +3 / Sharpness +2 / Clarity +1 ([T-Max P3200](../../../reference-recipes/kodak-t-max-p3200/), reference), Delta pulled back a notch on contrast and sharpness while keeping the same grain intensity and a similar light touch of Clarity.

## How the settings embody it
Acros carries real tonal gradation under the heavy grain overlay. Grain Strong/Large is the headline trait, heavier than every other B&W original here. DR400 holds the wide contrast range push development produces. Shadow +2 gives the deep, rich blacks extended development is known for, confirmed in the real scan. Clarity +1 (not 0) is a deliberate small deviation, some push-processed micro-contrast bite belongs in this look, though less than Tri-X's +3.
