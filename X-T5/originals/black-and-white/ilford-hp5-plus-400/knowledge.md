# Ilford HP5 Plus 400 — Grade Analysis & Validation

> Original (research + scan validated). See [`recipe.md`](recipe.md), the [B&W README](../README.md), and the [Knowledge base](../../../../Knowledge/README.md).

## The film
Ilford HP5 Plus 400 is the classic British medium-speed B&W — the reportage/documentary counterpart to Tri-X. It's known for **strong, balanced tonality, even (traditional cubic) grain that distributes smoothly, medium contrast, and deep shadows that stay dark** ("lets shadows be shadows"). Very forgiving and push-friendly — flexible across lighting.

## The look in one line
**Balanced, forgiving reportage B&W** — even visible grain, medium contrast, rich natural shadows.

## Real-scan validation (1 scan)
Analysed from Wikimedia Commons (analysis-only): a **foggy skyline** — prominent, even, structured grain across the smooth grey fog; medium contrast with buildings holding as solid dark-grey-to-black masses; gentle highlight roll-off in the mist. Confirmed: **even visible grain → Grain Strong/Small; balanced medium contrast → DR200 + Highlight 0; deep natural shadows → Shadow +2.** (Single scan → one more pass recommended for portrait/sunlit conditions.)

## vs the other B&W originals
HP5 is the **middle ground**:
- Grittier/heavier than [Acros](../fujifilm-acros-100/knowledge.md) (which is fine/smooth), but
- Smoother and lower-contrast than [Tri-X](../kodak-tri-x-400/knowledge.md) (which is clumpy-grained, high-contrast, Clarity +3).
HP5 keeps **Clarity 0** — it's a smooth, balanced stock, not a gritty one.

## Datasheet confirmation (Harman/Ilford HP5 Plus technical sheet, fetched & extracted)
- **"High speed, fine grain, medium contrast"** — the sheet's own words → confirms Highlight 0 (medium contrast) and grain visible-but-not-clumpy.
- **Push range documented to EI 3200/36** ("responds well to push-processing") → confirms the high ISO ceiling; grain/contrast rise gracefully with EI.
- Development aims produce "negatives of average contrast suitable for printing in all enlargers" → HP5's *balanced* curve is by design; our Shadow +2 carries the "lets shadows be shadows" depth without inflating overall contrast.
