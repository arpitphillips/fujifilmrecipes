About

A film simulation recipe bank for the Fujifilm X-T5, researched instead of guessed.

What this is

A recipe is a set of in camera JPEG settings that makes the X-T5 render like a specific film stock. Warm Kodak Gold afternoons. The cool green cast of a Superia print from a 90s minilab. Tri-X grain on a rainy street. All straight out of camera, with no editing afterwards.

Most recipe collections online are settings someone liked, with a film name attached after the fact. I wanted to work in the other direction: start from what the film actually was, then build the look.

Where the recipes come from

Film stocks are unusually well documented. Kodak and Ilford published technical datasheets for nearly everything: characteristic curves, granularity figures, colour balance, exposure latitude. Those numbers say precise things about how a film renders, and they map surprisingly well onto the X-T5's controls. A film with a long highlight shoulder wants DR400 and a negative Highlight setting. A film with RMS granularity of 14 has no business wearing fine grain.

So every original recipe here starts with the datasheet. When I can get scans of the real stock, from a lab roll or an archive, I compare them frame by frame against the recipe's output and adjust. The badge on each card tells you how far up that ladder a recipe has climbed. The validation methodology explains exactly what each tier means, and each recipe page keeps its grade analysis: the reasoning for every single setting, so you can bend it to your taste instead of obeying it.

Two shelves

Originals are the recipes I derived myself: colour film, cinema stocks, black and white, and a set of creative looks that don't pretend to be film at all. Community recipes come from the wider Fujifilm world. I keep those for testing and comparison, and every one is credited and linked to its creator's page.

Why straight out of camera

Fujifilm built its film simulations from measurements of its own emulsions, and the resulting colour behaviour is baked into the camera's processing in ways that curves and sliders can't honestly reproduce afterwards. I wrote a long piece on why. The short version: the JPEG the camera writes is the photograph. The recipe is your darkroom, and it runs in the half second after you press the shutter.

The fine print

Everything here targets the X-T5 and its X-Trans V sensor generation. On an X-Trans IV body, raise Color Chrome FX Blue one notch. The conversion guide covers the blue rendering change behind that rule.
